Objective

The initial objective was to provide beautifully displayed and easy-to-understand wind, wave, weather, and algae reports, forecasts, and statistics for sailors, surfers, divers, and any other water sports aficionados. During the initial stages of the project, I decided to concentrate on the surfers only, to allow for a quicker and more effective design process.

Challenges

The main challenges of this project were:

  • Enable users to get the right information at the right time.
  • Present complex information intuitively and cleanly.
  • Make the key features o the app as relevant as possible for surfers.
  • Interconnect key features in the best way possible, from a navigational and logical point of view.
  • Not fall victim to feature creep and try to squeeze in too many features (especially because I was a one-man team and had tight deadlines).

The Process

This project was structured around the Design Thinking process and backed up by the User-Centered Design process. True to their iterative nature I went back and forth through the steps, making sure all the design decisions were based on research and analysis.

1. Understand

Problem Statement and Solution

The first step was to define the problem to find a solution for it, and I came up with a problem statement:

Surfer users need a way to check meteorological information in real-time and to be able to quickly and accurately assess complex weather conditions.

The identified solution is designing a weather report app that:

  • Is visually appealing and contains a well-organized User Interface.
  • Makes checking the meteorological conditions a quick, effective, and delightful experience.
  • Decreases the possibility of incorrectly interpreting the data on weather conditions, or being prone to not checking them, because of it being complicated and unintuitive.

Competitor Analysis

I didn‘t go for the obvious ones: the biggest and most popular, instead I went for two smaller players: Surfline and Wisuki. Both are relatively new on the market and both have a task similar to my product's: new user acquisition and subsequently maintaining high engagement and retention rate.

The 2 competitors were analyzed by:

  1. Marketing Profile (the way they present themselves on the market and attract customers)
  2. Overall strategy ( their overall business strategy and position in respect to competitors)
  3. UX Analysis (the features they offer and overall usability)
  4. SWOT Analysis

The general target audience and competition were identified, as well as risks and opportunities. Also, all the goals and their KPIs were set in a time frame, to have fixed deadlines.

2. Observe

Research Goals

  • Better understand our potential users‘ mindset and behavior around the activity of surfing.
  • Discover which are the most important information our potential users need to find out about, and what tasks are they going to want to accomplish using our app.
  • Documenting user opinions ( both aspects that meet their needs and those that present pain points) on existing surfer dedicated weather web and mobile apps.
  • Determine how often and in which context our potential users would use the app.
  • Figure out how to get our potential users into the habit of engaging with our app before heading out to the water.

User Interviews

After establishing our Research Goals, it was time to start getting feedback from actual potential users through user interviews. User interviews were performed on three participants, all surfers with various levels of experience. The gathered insight and data were analyzed and organized using affinity mapping.

Quote: "To use and recommend an app, this app has to have something no other app has."

Research Hypotheses & Objectives

Hypothesis Objective
Hypothesis 1: We hypothesized that moving the Bank Selection step to the front would decrease user frustration and reduce cancellation rates by 50%. Objective 1: Decrease cancellation rate by 50% for users who have started the flow.
Hypothesis 2: Introducing a progress bar would increase the user's sense of control and lead to an improved conversion rate. Objective 2: Increase the conversion rate by improving user orientation.
Hypothesis 3: Reducing the number of steps would improve conversion rates. Objective 3: Increase conversion rate by simplifying the user flow.
Hypothesis 4: Fixing UI, UX, and copy issues would minimize user drop-off. Objective 4: Increase conversion and partner acceptance rates to 80%.

Usability Test Results & Findings

Usability Test Notes and Observations

The observations, participants' quotes and Errors and Usability issues were all gathered on the Figjam board and clustered directly after the session, together with the assisting person and the product owner in those cases where he was present. We would go through the notes that we made and discuss them.

Affinity Map

During this discussion sessions, we clustered all the relevant findings into common themes and issues using an Affinity Map. This helped us recognize and group the observations into 5 groups:

  • Errors / usability issues
  • Orientation and feeling of control of the flow process
  • UX copy and the presented information
  • Security and trust issues
  • Inclusiveness

Rainbow Spreadsheet

These observation points now grouped into common themes, were further put into a rainbow spreadsheet as a visual summmary and way to visually recognize patterns and see the severity of the problem or how common an observation is.

Key Findings Summmary

Here are the key findings from the usability test, which show that most of our assumptions have been confirmed:

  • Hypothesis 1 ✅

    4 out of 5 participants had an account with a bank that was not supported by Bank Ident (because they performed the task in test environment with testbank they could continue with the flow, but in real life they would be unable to proceed on the Bank-selection Screen) and all 5 expressed that they would prefer to have that screen at the begining so they don’t have to go through all the previous steps, invest so much time and effort into filling in input fields and reading about the steps ahead (Ident-Form & PIS-Intro screens) and then still be unable to finish the flow.

  • Hypothesis 2 ✅

    3 out of 5 participants Expressed a wish for some kind of progress indicator or progress bar, to be able to anticipate how many steps are ahead, to make them feel more comfortable and give them a feeling of control over the process, and make it more transparent. The remaining 2 participants were on one side an extremely tech savvy product manager, that very quickly finished the task, and had some background knowledge of the process, and therefore didn’t feel the need for a progress bar, and on the other a 60+ retired teacher, that wasn’t interested in the process, and how many steps it consists off, but her initial trust that she put into the process was insurance enough that she could trust it, and not worry how many steps it takes.

  • Hypothesis 3❓

    3 out of 5 participants didn’t read the text on the PIS-intro screen, and found the screen to be unnecessary, but the other 2 found it to be helpful. All the participants found that all the steps of the flow were necessary, and as the result, judging from the qualitative data and the perception of the test participants, this hypthesis has not been completely confirmed. What the UT showed is that the sense of orientation and control of the process i.e. knowing how many steps are left and having transparent information on what happens at each step is far more important for conversion.

  • Hypothesis 4 ✅

    A number of usability, UI, and UX issues were identified, including:

    1. Input Field Usability:
      • Date of Birth input field on the Ident-Form screen, and bank selection input on the Bank-Selection screen, were noted as problematic.
      • On smaller screens, if users fail to check the title radio button or make an error, the input field may not be visible, causing confusion when the "Next" button doesn’t respond.
    2. Button Placement:
      • The "Next" and "Back" buttons are too close and vertically aligned, leading to frequent misclicks. Three out of five participants clicked "Back" instead of "Next."
    3. Session Timeout:
      • Sessions timed out for four out of five participants, likely due to lengthy interaction with each screen. Adding a timeout warning or countdown could mitigate this issue.
    4. Confirmation Screen Data Layout:
      • Participants found separate screens for personal data on the QES-Confirm-Data screen inconvenient. Three out of five preferred viewing all data on a single screen for easier confirmation.
    5. Download Options on QES-Confirmation Screen:
      • Tech-savvy users suggested the document should be automatically saved, preferably emailed rather than downloaded to a local device.
      • Less tech-savvy users overlooked the toggle option, finding it irrelevant whether they received the document locally or not.
    6. UX Copy:
      • Some screens (e.g., PIS-Intro, QES-Confirm-Data) contained confusing or verbose language, with cryptic information and typographical errors, diminishing clarity.

Conclusion

The usability test confirmed that several changes could significantly improve the Bank Ident experience. Participants' feedback has highlighted the need for improved UI elements, clearer navigation, and an overall streamlined user experience.
