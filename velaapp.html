Objective

The initial objective was to provide beautifully displayed and easy-to-understand wind, wave, weather, and algae reports, forecasts, and statistics for sailors, surfers, divers, and any other water sports aficionados. During the initial stages of the project, I decided to concentrate on the surfers only, to allow for a quicker and more effective design process.

Challenges

The main challenges of this project were:

  • Enable users to get the right information at the right time.
  • Present complex information intuitively and cleanly.
  • Make the key features o the app as relevant as possible for surfers.
  • Interconnect key features in the best way possible, from a navigational and logical point of view.
  • Not fall victim to feature creep and try to squeeze in too many features (especially because I was a one-man team and had tight deadlines).

The Process

This project was structured around the Design Thinking process and backed up by the User-Centered Design process. True to their iterative nature I went back and forth through the steps, making sure all the design decisions were based on research and analysis.

1. Understand

Problem Statement and Solution

The first step was to define the problem to find a solution for it, and I came up with a problem statement:

Surfer users need a way to check meteorological information in real-time and to be able to quickly and accurately assess complex weather conditions.

The identified solution is designing a weather report app that:

  • Is visually appealing and contains a well-organized User Interface.
  • Makes checking the meteorological conditions a quick, effective, and delightful experience.
  • Decreases the possibility of incorrectly interpreting the data on weather conditions, or being prone to not checking them, because of it being complicated and unintuitive.

Competitor Analysis

I didn‘t go for the obvious ones: the biggest and most popular, instead I went for two smaller players: Surfline and Wisuki. Both are relatively new on the market and both have a task similar to my product's: new user acquisition and subsequently maintaining high engagement and retention rate.

The 2 competitors were analyzed by:

  1. Marketing Profile (the way they present themselves on the market and attract customers)
  2. Overall strategy ( their overall business strategy and position in respect to competitors)
  3. UX Analysis (the features they offer and overall usability)
  4. SWOT Analysis

Business requirements

Now that the goals for measuring the performance of the product itself were set, it was time to set the business goals and objectives. The general target audience and competition were identified, as well as risks and opportunities. Also, all the goals and their KPIs were set in a time frame, to have fixed deadlines.

2. Observe

Research Goals

  • Better understand our potential users‘ mindset and behavior around the activity of surfing.
  • Discover which are the most important information our potential users need to find out about, and what tasks are they going to want to accomplish using our app.
  • Documenting user opinions ( both aspects that meet their needs and those that present pain points) on existing surfer dedicated weather web and mobile apps.
  • Determine how often and in which context our potential users would use the app.
  • Figure out how to get our potential users into the habit of engaging with our app before heading out to the water.

User Interviews

After establishing our Research Goals, it was time to start getting feedback from actual potential users through user interviews. User interviews were performed on three participants, all surfers with various levels of experience. The gathered insight and data were analyzed and organized using affinity mapping.

Quote: "To use and recommend an app, this app has to have something no other app has."

Quote: "I am always looking at the image gallery of a surfing spot that I never visited, to get a feel of how the spot actually could be in real life."

Quote: "Surfers don't share information about less known spots gladly, they want to keep them somewhat secret because they don't want them to be too overcrowded."

User Interview Analysis

To better understand and organize all the information gathered during interviews, and analyze it, I made use of Affinity Mapping.
This method helped me come up with a list of key insights, that helped me decide upon what aspects and features of the app I am going to concentrate on in my project.

Key Insights:

  1. The surfers living near surfing spots tend not to go on long-distance surfing trips as often as surfers that don't have any surfing spots near the place they live.
  2. The more experienced surfers are, the more particular they are about the conditions they are looking for and interested in its details.
  3. The weather forecast is not particularly important to surfers, and algae reports have absolutely no relevance.
  4. Finding new spots is very important to surfers, regardless of their level of experience, but the more experienced they are, the more secretive they are about unknown and uncrowded surfing spots.
  5. There are two types of notifications relevant to surfers:
    • Notifications about a very particular combination of conditions depend on the surfer's personal preference and require a high level of detail manipulating notifications settings.
    • Warnings and alarms, for example in case of the presence of sharks or jellyfish at the surfing spot.

3. POV

Personas

Based on the data gathered through interviews and later analyzed, I set up two personas. They represented two major categories of users that we identified: beginner surfers and expert surfers. The two personas are 100% based on the interviewed participants in all their aspects relevant to the app.
The personas would help me to recognize what features I should concentrate on, and how the features would best resolve any need or problem a potential user might have.
I referred to them later on throughout the entire product development process.

User Journey Maps

With a list of the most relevant potential users' wants and needs, and possible scenarios in which those would occur, I devised 2 user journey maps to see how they would go about performing the tasks that would bring them to achieving their goals.

4. Ideate

User Flows

Now that the potential users were defined and had a solid enough shape, the moment came to work on ideas about how they would interact with the app. Although it was already clear by this time that the app would be relatively complex, with plenty of options available to the users, I wanted the user flows to stay simple and straightforward. This would keep the features that I would work on later in form of prototypes from becoming unnecessarily complicated.

Sitemap

The sitemap that resulted was somewhat complicated. The app itself has a complex information architecture because everything is so intertwined and connected, with child pages from different parents connected between themselves horizontally and between different levels in the hierarchy. But that is the nature of the app, and I wanted to allow the users to quickly access relevant screens that are not necessarily related to each other.

Card Sorting

To see if the sitemap I came up with would be intuitive and intelligible enough with potential users, I performed a closed card sorting. It was closed because the main categories were already quite clear and decided upon, and the only thing I wanted to find out was how the users would organize items, and see if their mental models would be aligned with the sitemap.

The card sorting was performed online on OptimalSort with 12 participants.

The results showed that the sitemap was pretty intuitive, and there were only two discrepancies:

  1. "Add Private Spot" card, which 9 out of 12 participants have placed in the "User Profile" category, and in the sitemap, it was under the "Search Spots" category.
  2. "Member Profile" is in the "Community" category in our sitemap, and all 12 participants have placed it in the "User Profile" category. Here it was obviously a matter of not labeling the card well enough, because from the name of the card it is not clear that it is about members, and not the user him/herself.

Consequently the structure of the sitemap remained the same, only the labeling of the features changed, but minimally.

5. Prototype

Hand-Drawn Sketches

To start off the prototyping phase of the project, I sketched wireframes for the navigation, both on mobile and desktop view, based on the sitemap. I began with the navigation so that I could start painting the app with the widest possible brush strokes, and have a base off of which I would develop the rest of the wireframes, and continue improving them.

After that I decided to sketch a couple of main features that I would work on throughout this project:

  1. Search surfing spots

    It was based on the same feature in one of the analyzed competitors but also heavily influenced by google maps, having similar functionality and design patterns. The users can find a surfing spot both by searching on the map and tapping on the pinpoint representing it or by typing its name in the search box.

  2. Search filter settings

    Being able to finely tune the filter settings for the search feature is very important, especially because the beginner and expert surfers have different preferences.

  3. Notifications settings

    Set up notifications to get both in-app and push notifications about the particular conditions that the users themselves defined.

High Fidelity Wireframes

Find and Save Secret Surfing Spots Feature

The animation shows the "Secret Spot" feature. The first iteration of it was based on a similar feature found in one of the analyzed competitors. Tapping on the FAB button opens a pop-up and a pin appears centered on the screen. The users swipe the map underneath, getting dynamically the location underneath the pin on the pop-up. When the users find the right location, pressing the CTA brings them to the Add Secret Spot Screen, where they can give the spot a custom name and add info about it. Clicking on the CTA takes them to the newly created surfing spot screen.

Filter Settings for the Search Feature

To reduce the cognitive load, using the principle of progressive disclosure, the advanced settings are hidden by default. The users can twirl them open if they want to access those. Translated to user personas, the assumption was that this would be valuable to the more experienced surfers, while the beginners would probably only use the basic settings. I would be sure about that only after performing usability testing though.

Notifications Settings Screen

Starts with the empty state screen containing starter content in form of an example notification. This way the users know that the list of notifications will appear on this screen and the extended FAB button that takes us to the new notification screen. Similar to the filter settings screen, the parameters here are all closed off as default and can be twirled open to prevent cognitive overload.

6. Test

Usability Testing

Moderated usability tests were performed on 6 participants remotely. All of the participants were found through my personal network. They were sent the link to the prototype. Zoom was used for communicating with them, webcam so I can see their faces, and they also shared their screen while interacting with the prototype. Sessions lasted on average between 20-3 0min.

Usability Test Tasks:

  1. Find and save a secret surfing spot.
  2. Set the filter options so you see only search results inside of a 50km radius and for your level of surfing proficiency (beginner-expert).
  3. Set the Notifications Options, so you get notified about when the conditions are perfect at the surfing spot of your choice.

Objectives:

  1. Observe how well the users navigate the app, and also if all the CTAs are easy to figure out.
  2. Determine how easy it is for the users to find surfing spots and see the forecast and all the info about it that is useful to them.
  3. Determine the same about the aforementioned core features.
  4. Measure the amount of errors participants commit, and which are the most common ones.

Test Results & Analysis

To analyze the data obtained through user testing I performed another affinity mapping. The results showed me that there were no catastrophic usability errors. A couple of issues were caused by the prototype itself as opposed to the actual app's functionality. To categorize and rank the severity of the errors, I used an adapted version of Jakob Nielsen’s four-step rating scale.

The Rainbow Spreadsheet helped me draw out possible solutions from the newly organized data by identifying patterns in participants' input.
The participants were also coming up with very creative and interesting ideas, for example sharing secret spots with other users, or pinpoints on the map for other kinds of sport or leisure activities, not only surfing spots. Some of these observations I had taken into consideration and would implement later in the following iterations.

Test Report and Fixing the Issues

Based on the evidence from the usability test and rainbow spreadsheet, I identified 4 top errors and issues that needed to be addressed in the next iteration of my prototype.

1. Find Secret Spot Functionality too confusing for the Users

Finding and saving secret surfing spots is one of the core features of the app, but it posed quite a problem for all the participants.

Severity: High

Evidence: 4 participants found the feature's functionality and flow to be confusing and not intuitive, one participant was unable to initiate the task alone, couldn't recognize the FAB button, and needed help, 3 participants were unable to perform the task without suggestions and 1 participant expressed the wish to find the location typing in the name or coordinates, and not only by moving the pinpoint on the map.

Solution: Completely change the functionality of the feature, going for something the users would be more familiar with. Since most users are familiar with Google Maps, we decided to use the mental model already created by it for this feature, and try to replicate the functionality found there. We decided to get rid of the FAB button, and let the user tap and hold to open a modal with info about the location, and the possibility to save the location as a secret spot. It is also possible to come to the same result by typing the name of the location in the search box.

2. Save Button missing on the Search Filter Settings

The results showed that the sitemap was pretty intuitive, and there were only two discrepancies:Here everything proved to be working fine, except the fact that the participants were looking for the save button, which I overlooked, thinking that after adjusting the settings the users would just press the back arrow to go back to the map view.

Severity: Medium

Evidence: 5 participants were trying to find the save button after adjusting the filter settings and couldn't find it, and were not sure how to go back to the map view with the settings saved.

Solution: I added the "Save Settings" button at the bottom of the screen.

3. Adding Surfing Spots to Favorites Functionality Missing

A folder for the favorite spots existed already. Unfortunately, we completely forgot to make it possible for users to add spots to the favorites folder.

Severity: High

Evidence: 5 participants were perplexed by this problem and although it was not explicitly part of any of the tasks, they asked what the favorites are, and how to save spots to favorites.

Solution: After consulting competitors to see how this problem was dealt with, I chose a heart icon next to the spot name to be the best solution for this problem.

4. No Way of Finding Secret Spots Inside the App Once They are Saved

Saving Secret Spots that are only visible to the user that saved them, and get a forecast for them, is another of the core features of the app. Although we elaborated a way to search and save them, there was no way of finding these spots later.

Severity: High

Evidence: 5 participants asked where to find the secret spots screens after they have been saved.

Solution: I found that the folder with a list of secret spots should be inside of the user category, and we placed it in the first tab of the user screen, directly under the surfing history list.

7.Refining the Design

After adjusting the prototype a less official and extensive round of usability testing was performed on it, along with peer review, where I got some valuable input from my course's colleagues, and both my tutor and mentor. This resulted in a couple of new iterations with improved usabilty. Parallely, I started polishing the UI, aiming for an overall consistency both in terms of visual design and design patterns. This involved for example devising a grid for the screens and developing a color scheme. The result was a Styleguide for my app.

Grid

On the example of the Surfing Spot screen, we can see how introducing a grid to our screens has improved the organization of the content and elements of the screen layouts. The grid helped us to align all the entries to the left, touching the margin. The heart icon that serves to save the spot to favorites has been centered inside the fifth columns, and the arrow back is also aligned left to the margin. the tabs are maybe not obviously aligned to the grid, but the grid helped us find the middle of the screen, to find where the tab line is ending. We also aligned the spot's name to the left on the second column.

Color Scheme

The color scheme also through several iterations until I came to the final solution. I was very aware of the importance and various implication of the color choices, and of the fact that they not only visually represent the app's identity, but are also very efficient from the point of view of the emotional and subconscious effect they have ( visceral level).

Conclusion

Plans for the future would be doing a couple of more iterations and subsequent Usability tests, and only go into the development phase when I am sure that any major technical debt would be avoided. This might last for another 2 months. I also have to be careful not to allow for feature creep, and not go too wild trying to improve the product, so both time limits and technical limits of what is possible are met.
